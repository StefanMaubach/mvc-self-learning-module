Self-Learning Module: Multivariable Calculus

Exercise 12

$S$ is the area enclosed by the four lines: $y=0$, $y=1$, $y=x$, $y=x-2$. Integrate $f(x,y)=xy$ over $S$.

Make a picture, and from there plan along. (There is deliberately no picture in this exercise.)

Figure out what the bounds are, and integrate along those bounds. You'll have (several) $\int\int \ldots d\ldots d\ldots$ .

You may have an integral consisting of several pieces looking like $ \int_{x=\ldots}^{\ldots} \int_{y=\ldots}^{\ldots} f(x,y) dy dx$. You may also have $ \int_{y=\ldots}^{\ldots} \int_{x=\ldots}^{\ldots} f(x,y) dx dy$. Which one of these two do you prefer? (Its instructive to at least figure out the bounds for both).

If you put $x$ first (outside), then you need to separate into three parts: $0\leq x\leq 1$ (a triangle), $1\leq x\leq 2$ (a rectangle/square) and $2\leq x\leq 3$ (a different triangle).
If you look at the first triangle, you see that here the top bound is the line $y=x$, and the bottom bound is $y=0$. This gives $\int_{x=0}^{x=1}\int_{y=0}^{y=x} f(x,y) dy dx$.
In the square the bounds are $y=0$ and $y=1$ giving $\int_{x=1}^{x=2}\int_{y=0}^{y=1} f(x,y) dy dx$.
In the last triangle the bounds are $y=x-2$ and $y=1$ giving $\int_{x=2}^{x=3}\int_y={x-2}^{y=1} f(x,y) dy dx$.
So in total you get $$ \int_{x=0}^{x=1}\int_{y=0}^{y=x} f(x,y) dy dx +\int_{x=1}^{x=2}\int_{y=0}^{y=1} f(x,y) dy dx+\int_{x=2}^{x=3}\int_{y=x-2}^{y=1} f(x,y) dy dx .$$ However, what if you swap the roles of $y$ and $x$, and get $ \int_{y=\ldots}^{\ldots} \int_{x=\ldots}^{\ldots} f(x,y) dx dy$ ?

Then you integrate for $y=0$ to $y=1$ (all of the figure is within these two vertical values), but what is nice is that the horizontal bounds are the same everywhere: $y=x$ (or $x=y$) and $y=x-2$ (or $x=y+2$). This makes the integration easier, as you just have one part: $$ \int_{y=0}^{y=1}\int_{x=y}^{x=y+2} f(x,y) dx dy. $$ Now wrap it up.

$$ \int_{y=0}^{y=1}\int_{x=y}^{x=y+2} xy dx dy = \int_{y=0}^1 \left[ \frac{x^2}{2}y \right]_{x=y}^{x=y+2} dy =\int_{y=0}^1 \frac{(y+2)^2y}{2}-\frac{y^3}{2}dy= \int_{y=0}^1 2y^2+2y dy = \left[ \frac{2y^3}{3} +y\right]_{y=0}^1=\frac{2}{3}+1=\frac{5}{3}.$$
